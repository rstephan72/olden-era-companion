    Neutral Magic¶

    The schoolless school — utility spells every hero can eventually use, belonging to no scholar and no faction. Almost entirely adventure-map tools: teleports, logistics, mana tricks, and two combat exceptions. Nobody builds around Neutral Magic; everybody uses it.

    How Neutral spells work (read this first — they're different)¶

    Neutral spells break every rule the other four schools follow:

    1. Unlocked by hero level, not Mage Guilds. Tiers 1–5 unlock at hero levels 1, 3, 6, 9, and 12 respectively. Every hero gets them on schedule, no buildings required.
    2. Leveled by a points system, not spell scrolls. Neutral spells upgrade automatically as you accumulate points from normal play: unlocking a normal spell = 1 point, leveling a normal spell = 1 point, constructing a building = 2 points, building a Mage Guild = 6 points. In practice: playing the game levels them for you — a developed empire has high-level Neutral spells without ever choosing them.
    3. No Masterful versions exist for any Neutral spell — no hero specializes in them, so this chapter has no Masterful sections.
    4. Several cost 0 mana, which is not a typo.

    Battle examples (for the two combat spells) use the standard reference army — 20 Onyx Dancers (18 HP each, ~120 damage/volley) — at Spellpower 10. A sourcing honesty note: the wiki's table lists no per-level upgrades for several Tier 1 spells and for Summon Avatar (which instead comes in three named ranks); where per-level data is absent below, that absence is stated rather than invented.

    Tier 1 (hero level 1)¶

    Magic Arrow¶

    Mana cost: 0

    ⭐ 3/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 1/5 · 📈 Early 4/5 · Mid 2/5 · Late 1/5

    Plain English: A free bolt of damage. Zero mana. Every hero, every battle, from level 1.

    The Math: Damage = 25 + (5 × Spellpower) → 75 damage at Spellpower 10 (and a still-useful ~35 at a level-1 hero's typical SP 2). The wiki lists no per-level upgrades for this spell.

    In battle: 75 damage kills 4 Dancer-class units — a third of your reference stack's volley — for nothing. That price changes hero design: even the most spell-averse Might hero contributes chip damage every single round of every fight, forever. Early game, versus tiny neutral stacks, Magic Arrow alone can carry fights your Week 1 army couldn't otherwise take without losses.

    Why you'd cast it: It's free. The question is why you wouldn't — and the answer is only "because a better spell exists this turn."

    When it's weak: Mid-game onward, 75 damage stops mattering; it becomes the thing you cast when nothing else is worth mana.

    Common mistakes: Forgetting it exists on Might heroes — free damage is free.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★☆☆ (3/5) — the floor of every hero's turn is never "pass," and that's worth more than it looks.

    Necromancer Will¶

    Mana cost: 0

    ⭐ 2/5 (5/5 for Necropolis) · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 2/5 · 📈 all stages: faction-dependent

    Plain English: A toggle switch for Necromancy — turn undead-raising off, and your hero banks Necromantic Energy after victories instead of raising troops.

    The Math: No formula; a resource-routing toggle. The wiki lists no per-level upgrades.

    Why you'd cast it: Pure Necropolis infrastructure: when your army is full or the available raise is low-value trash units, banking the energy for later (or better) conversion is strictly superior to overflowing. For all other factions it's a dead page in the spellbook.

    When it's weak: It isn't weak or strong — it's a settings menu. Non-Necropolis heroes simply never touch it.

    Common mistakes: Necropolis players leaving raising on autopilot and diluting armies with unwanted skeleton-tier units.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★☆☆☆ (2/5) overall — but effectively a Necropolis faction mechanic wearing a spell's clothes; rated 5/5 inside that faction chapter.

    Back to Town!¶

    Mana cost: 10

    ⭐ 3/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 2/5 · 📈 Early 3/5 · Mid 3/5 · Late 3/5

    Plain English: Emergency evacuation — teleports your hero to the nearest owned city within 16 squares. All remaining movement is forfeited. Once per day.

    The Math: Fixed 16-square range, 1/day. The wiki lists no per-level upgrades.

    Why you'd cast it: Two uses define it: escape (an enemy hero is one day away and your army isn't ready) and logistics (end the day's exploring, teleport home, spend the night's gold on troops, rehire, and march out re-armed). The movement forfeit prices it honestly — it's an end-of-turn button, so cast it after spending the day's moves, never before.

    When it's weak: More than 16 squares from home, it's a blank page — deep expeditions travel beyond its reach.

    Common mistakes: Casting mid-day and torching half a turn of movement for no reason.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★☆☆ (3/5) — insurance plus a weekly-rhythm logistics tool, free of skill investment.

    Mana Rite¶

    Mana cost: 0

    ⭐ 3/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 3/5 · 📈 Early 3/5 · Mid 3/5 · Late 3/5

    Plain English: Trades the rest of today's movement for mana — 1% of your maximum mana per 1% of movement remaining.

    The Math: Mana restored = (remaining movement %) of max mana. Stand still all day: full mana bar. The wiki lists no per-level upgrades.

    Why you'd cast it: Mana is the binding constraint on caster heroes, and this converts a resource you sometimes don't need (movement on a rest day) into the one you always do. The play pattern: the day before a big planned assault, move only as far as the staging square, then Rite the remainder into a full tank.

    When it's weak: Every cast costs map tempo — used carelessly it's paying gold-equivalent time for mana you'd regenerate anyway.

    Common mistakes: Casting with 10% movement left for 10% mana — the trade only shines when the movement was going spare.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★☆☆ (3/5) — a tempo-for-power exchange that rewards players who plan their assault days.

    Relocation¶

    Mana cost: 12

    ⭐ 4/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 2/5 · 📈 Early 3/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 4/5

    Plain English: Remotely opens any city you control and swaps troops with it — reinforcement without the walk. Once per day.

    The Math: Any controlled city, map-wide, 1/day. The wiki lists no per-level upgrades.

    Why you'd cast it: This is the quiet economy engine of the school. The weekly rhythm of Heroes games is "new troops appear in town; hero marches back to collect them" — Relocation deletes the march. Every week, from anywhere: 12 mana, and this week's growth teleports into your army. Over a campaign that's days of saved movement, which compounds into more fights, more experience, more map.

    When it's weak: Single-city, single-hero situations shrink it to a convenience.

    Common mistakes: Forgetting the 1/day limit when trying to shuffle troops across multiple cities.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★★☆ (4/5) — the best logistics spell most players never consciously chose; it levels itself via the points system while you play.

    Tier 2 (hero level 3)¶

    Pocket Dimension¶

    Mana cost: 14

    ⭐ 2/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 4/5 · 📈 Early 1/5 · Mid 2/5 · Late 3/5

    Plain English: Opens a door to a private stash-space on the map — any allied hero can bank creatures and artifacts inside. The door closes at day's end (and vanishes if an enemy touches it).

    The Math:

    Level Effect
    L1 Entrance on adjacent hex, lasts to end of day, 1/day
    L2 Mana 14 → 10
    L3 Entrance placeable within 4 squares
    L4 Lasts until end of next day

    All four levels: - L1: Creates a Remote Foothold entrance on an adjacent space until day's end; allied heroes can store creatures and artifacts. An enemy interacting with it destroys it. Once per day. - L2: Mana cost drops from 14 to 10. - L3: Entrance can be created within 4 squares. - L4: Entrance persists through the next day.

    Why you'd cast it: Sophisticated multi-hero plays: parking a reserve army mid-map where a second hero can collect it, or stashing artifacts before a risky fight (a defeated hero drops what they carry — a stash means losing the battle without losing the loadout).

    When it's weak: Solo-hero, low-stakes play has nothing to stash and nobody to share with.

    Common mistakes: Stashing next to an enemy's path — their touch deletes the door, with your goods behind it.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★☆☆☆ (2/5) — a genuinely clever tool that most campaigns never quite need.

    Second Wind¶

    Mana cost: 17

    ⭐ 4/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 2/5 · 📈 Early 3/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 4/5

    Plain English: Instantly refunds movement points — your hero simply walks further today. The map-tempo spell.

    The Math:

    Level Movement restored Mana Uses/day
    L1 40 17 1
    L2 40 13 1
    L3 60 13 1
    L4 60 13 2

    All four levels: - L1: Immediately restores 40 Movement points. Once per day. - L2: Mana cost drops from 17 to 13. - L3: Restoration rises from 40 to 60. - L4: Usable twice per day.

    Why you'd cast it: Movement is the true currency of the adventure map — every extra square is more resources, earlier fights, faster snowball. L4's two casts of 60 is a daily +120 movement subsidy, which over a week amounts to a free extra day of travel. This is the inverse of Mana Rite: that spell sells movement for mana; this buys it back. Casters with spare mana should almost always be net buyers.

    When it's weak: Sieges, garrison duty, any parked hero — movement restored to a hero who isn't moving is nothing.

    Common mistakes: Hoarding the daily use "for later" and ending days with it uncast — unused charges are the only way this spell fails.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★★☆ (4/5) — map tempo in a bottle; one of the most quietly winning spells in the game.

    Tier 3 (hero level 6)¶

    Summon Avatar¶

    Mana cost: 0 · comes in three ranks (Basic / Advanced / Expert) rather than four levels

    ⭐ 4/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 2/5 · 📈 Early 3/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 4/5

    Plain English: Summons your hero's Avatar — a free, personal battlefield champion whose strength is built from your hero's own stats. One per side per battle. (This spell is also the subject of its own Magic skill, "Summon Avatar," which upgrades the rank.)

    The Math: The Avatar's statline scales with Spellpower on both HP and damage, plus Attack/Defense equal to your Spellpower and Knowledge. Worked at Spellpower 10:

    Rank HP formula Damage formula At SP 10
    Basic 100 + 12×SP 5 + 6×SP 220 HP, 65 dmg
    Advanced 100 + 18×SP 5 + 12×SP 280 HP, 125 dmg
    Expert 100 + 24×SP 5 + 18×SP 340 HP, 185 dmg

    In battle: An Expert Avatar at Spellpower 10 walks in with 340 HP — nearly a full 20-Dancer stack's durability (360) — and swings for 185, half again more than the entire Dancer volley — for zero mana, every battle, permanently. It's a sixth army slot conjured from your hero sheet. And because every stat in its formula is hero-derived, it keeps pace with your hero forever: at SP 20 late-game, the Expert Avatar is a 580-HP, 365-damage monster. The one-per-side cap and its summon-type vulnerability (Anti-Magic deletes it) are the only brakes.

    Ranks (in place of the usual four levels): - Basic: 100 + (12 × SP) HP; 5 + (6 × SP) base damage; gains Attack and Defense equal to the hero's Spellpower and Knowledge. - Advanced: HP rate 12 → 18; damage rate 6 → 12. - Expert: HP rate 18 → 24; damage rate 12 → 18.

    Masterful version: None — like all Neutral spells. Rank progression via the Summon Avatar skill fills the same role.

    Why you'd cast it: Free stats every fight; the closest thing to a seventh creature slot in the game.

    Best heroes: Magic heroes, whose Spellpower and Knowledge feed every line of the formula twice.

    When it's weak: On low-SP Might heroes the Avatar arrives as a speed bump; and anti-summon tech deletes it like any conjured stack.

    Common mistakes: Not casting it every single battle — a free summon uncast is the purest wasted value in the game.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★★☆ (4/5) — the best reason the Neutral school exists; on a dedicated Magic hero it borders on 5/5.

    Shadowflight¶

    Mana cost: 20

    ⭐ 3/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 3/5 · 📈 Early 1/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 4/5

    Plain English: Your hero flies until the end of the day — over mountains, forests, rivers, and anything else the map put in the way.

    The Math:

    Level Effect Mana
    L1 Fly until day's end; obstacle-crossing costs +30% movement 20
    L2 Same 14
    L3 Obstacle-crossing costs nothing extra 14
    L4 -20% movement cost while flying 14

    All four levels: - L1: Grants flight until the end of the day; moving above obstacles costs +30% movement. - L2: Mana cost drops from 20 to 14. - L3: Obstacle-crossing surcharge removed. - L4: All movement while flying costs -20%.

    Masterful version: None.

    Why you'd cast it: Maps are mazes; flight is the answer key. Cutting across a mountain range that would cost three days of walking, skipping a guarded chokepoint entirely, dropping onto an enemy's undefended town from an "impossible" direction — Shadowflight converts map geometry from constraint into suggestion. At L4 it's not just obstacle-proof, it's a net movement discount — strictly better travel.

    When it's weak: Open flat maps have nothing to fly over; the spell is only as good as the terrain is bad.

    Common mistakes: Casting at day's end with 10 movement left — it expires at midnight; cast at dawn, fly all day.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★☆☆ (3/5) — map-dependent, and on the right map it trivializes route-planning entirely.

    Town Portal¶

    Mana cost: 24

    ⭐ 5/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 2/5 · 📈 Early 2/5 · Mid 5/5 · Late 5/5

    Plain English: Teleports your hero to any city you control — your choice, any distance. The most famous logistics spell in the series, and it earns the reputation here too.

    The Math:

    Level Effect Mana Uses/day
    L1 Teleport to any owned city 24 1
    L2 Same 18 1
    L3 Same 18 2
    L4 Same 18 3

    All four levels: - L1: Teleports the casting hero to a selected controlled city. Once per day. - L2: Mana cost drops from 24 to 18. - L3: Usable twice per day. - L4: Usable three times per day.

    Masterful version: None.

    Why you'd cast it: Town Portal is why late-game empires are defensible at all. The eternal dilemma — push the offensive or rush home to defend — stops existing when home is 18 mana away: push and defend, same hero, same day. At L4's three daily casts, one hero garrisons an entire empire: defend city A in the morning, reinforce at city B midday, and portal back to the front by nightfall. Paired with Relocation (grab fresh troops remotely) and Second Wind (movement after arrival), the Neutral school quietly contains the entire logistics engine of a winning campaign.

    When it's weak: One-city starts get a discount escape rope and nothing more — its power scales exactly with your empire.

    Common mistakes: Learning it late. The points system levels it passively, but the moment it matters is the moment you own a second city — plan around having it by then.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★★★ (5/5) — the most important non-combat spell in the game, precisely as veterans of the series would predict.

    Tier 4 (hero level 9)¶

    Dimension Door¶

    Mana cost: 22

    ⭐ 4/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 3/5 · 📈 Early 1/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 4/5

    Plain English: Short-range teleport to any visible spot nearby — no city required, no path required. Blink for the adventure map.

    The Math:

    Level Range Mana Uses/day
    L1 8 squares 22 1
    L2 8 14 1
    L3 11 14 1
    L4 11 14 2

    All four levels: - L1: Teleports the hero to a target location within 8 squares. Once per day. - L2: Mana cost drops from 22 to 14. - L3: Range rises from 8 to 11 squares. - L4: Usable twice per day.

    Masterful version: None.

    Why you'd cast it: Where Town Portal is strategic, this is tactical: hop the river without the bridge, skip the guard sitting on the only road, land behind the enemy hero who thought the mountain protected them. Historically this spell breaks Heroes games — its once-per-day cap and modest range here are the balance leash, and even leashed, "ignore any 11 squares of map, twice a day" wins races that decide campaigns.

    When it's weak: Corridors and open plains where the straight walk was fine anyway.

    Common mistakes: Spending it to save 3 squares of ordinary walking — it's a key, not a stroll; save it for doors.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★★☆ (4/5) — the tactical teleport, held one star below its legendary ancestors purely by the daily cap.

    Gate of Light¶

    Mana cost: 28

    ⭐ 3/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 4/5 · 📈 Early 1/5 · Mid 3/5 · Late 4/5

    Plain English: Opens a portal that anyone on your side can walk through until day's end — not just the caster. A door for your whole roster of heroes.

    The Math:

    Level Exit range Mana Uses/day
    L1 Within 5 squares of caster 28 1
    L2 5 20 1
    L3 7 20 1
    L4 7 20 2

    All four levels: - L1: Creates a portal lasting until day's end; any hero may enter from an adjacent space and exit within 5 squares of the caster. Once per day. - L2: Mana cost drops from 28 to 20. - L3: Exit radius rises from 5 to 7 squares. - L4: Usable twice per day.

    Masterful version: None.

    Why you'd cast it: The multi-hero force multiplier: your main caster opens the gate at the front line, and the slow secondary hero hauling this week's reinforcements steps through from home territory — whole armies skipping the map. It's Dimension Door's answer to the question "but what about everyone else?" The skill ceiling is coordination: the gate's value equals how well your other heroes were positioned to use it today, before it closes at midnight.

    When it's weak: Solo-hero play can't use a door built for a convoy.

    Common mistakes: Opening it with no second hero in position — a 28-mana door nobody walks through.

    Campaign Verdict: ★★★☆☆ (3/5) — niche solo, excellent in multi-hero economies; the ceiling belongs to planners.

    Reading the school as a whole¶

    The Neutral school is the game's logistics layer wearing a spellbook's covers. Its combat contribution is two spells (Magic Arrow's free chip, Summon Avatar's free champion) — everything else is about where your heroes are and what they're carrying: three teleports of ascending scale (Back to Town! → Dimension Door/Gate of Light → Town Portal), two resource converters (Mana Rite, Second Wind), remote logistics (Relocation, Pocket Dimension), and one faction toggle. Because the points system levels these passively while you build and learn other spells, a well-developed empire gets an elite logistics suite for free — the practical advice is simply to remember it's there. The players who consciously weave Town Portal + Relocation + Second Wind into their weekly rhythm run functionally faster campaigns than players fielding identical armies who don't.

    Common beginner mistakes (school-wide)¶

    • Never opening the Neutral tab at all — the points system means these spells quietly leveled themselves while you weren't looking.
    • Forgetting Summon Avatar exists and fighting every battle a free champion short.
    • Casting movement/teleport spells at day's end when they pay at dawn.
    • Undervaluing Relocation and Town Portal because "logistics isn't strategy" — in this genre, logistics is most of it.
