    Necropolis — Shadowspire's Endless Dead¶

    ⭐ Power: 4/5 · 🟢 Beginner Friendly: 4/5 · ⚔️ Campaign: 5/5 · 💰 Economy: 4/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 3/5 · 📈 Early 3/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 5/5

    In one sentence: The attrition faction — every battle you win makes your army bigger through Necromancy, your units drain strength from enemies as they fight, and given enough time you simply cannot be out-grown.

    Identity (per the official faction page): ruled by the Necromancers' Guild of Shadowspire; undead; keywords Strong Debuffs & Curses and Improving army by raising the Dead. Community consensus rates it the best late-game faction and, alongside Dungeon, one of the two easiest to win with — the snowball takes care of itself.

    Faction skill: Necromancy¶

    Known by all Necropolis hero classes (Death Knights and their magic counterparts), unavailable elsewhere.

    What it does (official mechanics, verified): After winning a battle, transforms 20% of the strongest enemy stack into friendly Undead of the same tier. This consumes Necromantic Energy, which stores up to 1,500 and refills each week (or when the hero has no stack of that tier). Crucially, Necromancy works on all creatures, not just the Undead — every living enemy is future army. Documented subskills include: native-terrain bonus (+10% dealt / -10% taken on Deathland), +4 mana per battle victory, and a granted ability that heals a friendly Undead stack and revives its fallen units. The Necromancer class additionally spawns a temporary friendly Wight stack whenever an enemy stack dies in battle.

    The companion tool — Necromancer Will (Neutral spell, 0 mana): toggles raising off, banking Necromantic Energy instead. This is the skill's hidden depth: raising junk-tier troops dilutes a refined army, so experienced players toggle off against low-value enemies and bank the energy for better conversions. See the Neutral Magic chapter's entry — it's rated 5/5 for this faction specifically.

    Why it defines the faction: every other faction pays gold to grow; Necropolis grows by fighting. The correct Necropolis campaign is a deliberately high-volume one — take every fight the map offers, because each one is a recruitment event.

    Unit roster (full stats, verified against the official wiki)¶

    Native terrain: Deathland. All units are Undead creature-type.

    Tier 1: Skeleton → Skeleton Warrior / Skeleton Archer¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Growth Notes
    Skeleton 35g 6 4/1 1–3 4 3 30/wk Melee
    Skeleton Warrior 50g 6 6/3 2–3 6 3 30 Melee
    Skeleton Archer 50g 6 4/1 1–3 4 3 30 Ranged

    The cheapest units in the game (35g!) at the game's highest growth rate (30/week) — before Necromancy adds more. Skeleton Archer is the faction-defining pick: a ranged stack that grows every battle is the engine everything else protects.

    Tier 2: Wight → Wraith / Phantasm¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Notes
    Wight 105g 9 2/0 2–4 6 3 Flying; Memory Devourer I (20% dmg dealt → HP, can create temp units)
    Wraith 140g 9 6/0 2–4 7 4 MD II (40%); Howl of the Banshee (forces enemy to random adjacent hex)
    Phantasm 140g 9 6/4 2–4 7 4 MD I + Nightmares (stacking 1-pure-dmg curse on hit)

    The sustain identity starts here: Wights convert damage into extra temporary units. Wraith for the displacement utility (shoving enemies off chokepoints); Phantasm for durability + curse chip.

    Tier 3: Undead Pet → Barghest / Armored Hound¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Notes
    Undead Pet 120g 14 4/6 3–5 5 5 Melee Defense I (-30% melee taken)
    Barghest 160g 14 8/6 3–5 8 6 + Bone Crusher (steals 1 Def per strike, undispellable) + Call of the Pack (summons 20% of stack)

    Correction from the roster-tree draft: Undead Pet is the T3 base, upgrading into Barghest or Armored Hound. The community hype around this line is earned — -30% melee taken on the whole line makes it the best cheap screen in the game. Barghest for tempo (Init 8, Spd 6, self-summoning); Armored Hound for the anvil.

    Tier 4: Graverobber → Merchant of Death / Kennelmaster¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Notes
    Graverobber 260g 30 13/9 7–9 2 3 Long Reach; Dig Up Bones (summons 2 Skeletons per unit!)
    Merchant of Death 360g 30 13/9 7–9 5 4 + stacking poison on attacks + Ensnare Memories (summons Wights)
    Kennelmaster 360g 30 13/13 8–10 4 4 + Fetch (summons Undead Pets) + Hail! (marks enemy: always takes max damage)

    The glue line: every version manufactures more army mid-fight. A 20-Graverobber stack conjures 40 free Skeletons per activation. Kennelmaster's Hail! is Masterful-Vulnerability-as-a-creature-ability — extraordinary on a T4.

    Tier 5: Lich → Pestilent Lich / Sanguine Lich¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Notes
    Lich 320g 50 12/12 11–16 4 2 Ranged; Rewind Death (heals 20 HP/lich, revives dead)
    Pestilent Lich 450g 50 17/12 16 fixed 5 3 Area Strike (splash on every attack) + Death's Embrace (detonates all effects on target at full remaining value)
    Sanguine Lich 450g 50 12/16 11–16 4 3 Unlife, Undeath (20% of dmg dealt heals whole army) + 10% bonus XP + Rewind Death at 30 HP/lich

    The ranged carry. Pestilent is the damage pick (AoE volleys + a devastating combo with your own stacking curses via Death's Embrace); Sanguine turns the stack into an army-wide sustain engine. Both carry creature-level resurrection — Rewind Life as an ability, no spellbook required.

    Tier 6: Dread Knight → Avatar of War / Hollow Reaper¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Notes
    Dread Knight 800g 100 19/18 12–14 9 6 Double Strike; -1 enemy morale aura
    Avatar of War 1300g 100 19/20 18–20 11 7 Double Strike; -2 morale aura; steals 1 Atk per strike (undispellable)
    Hollow Reaper 1300g 100 24/18 22–24 10 6 -2 morale aura; +3 Atk per friendly death; Death Stare: kills +1 unit per attack, up to tier 7

    The community's "six Dread Knights beat ten Temple elites" reputation checks out in the numbers: Double Strike means Avatar of War effectively swings 36–40 per unit. Hollow Reaper trades Double Strike for a guaranteed bonus kill per attack — the anti-elite pick.

    Tier 7: Vampire → Vampire Lord / Vampire Scholar¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Notes
    Vampire 1200g + 1 Mercury 150 25/20 25 fixed 11 5 Flying; Vampirism (40% of dmg dealt → HP, revives fallen)
    Vampire Lord 2000g + 2 Mercury 175 25/27 30 13 7 + Swift Strike (no enemy counterattacks, ever)
    Vampire Scholar 2000g + 2 Mercury 150 27/20 30 12 5 Long Reach; Blood Transfusion (drain enemy → heal/revive ally) + Corpse Explosion

    The faction thesis as a T7: a flying lifedrinker whose stack refills itself and revives its own dead mid-combat. Vampire Lord is the front-line monster (attacks that are never punished + 40% drain = near-unkillable in sustained melee); Scholar is the support pick, converting enemy HP into ally revival. Note the Mercury cost — this faction's rare-resource bottleneck.

    Recommended magic schools (cross-referenced)¶

    Primary: Nightshade. The faction text says "Strong Debuffs & Curses" and the school delivers exactly that; better, undead immunity to several Nightshade tools (Sleep can't target your own troops in mirror situations) means you suffer the school's downsides less than anyone. Key entries: Despair (stackable DoT — undead armies grind long fights where it shines), Vulnerability, and Web — specialist hero Marl (Death Knight) applies his from the start of battle, the perfect screen for a skeleton-archer core.

    Primary co-pick: Arcane, for one spell — Rewind Life. Lord Rufus's Masterful version revives units beyond their original amount, which on top of Necromancy makes Necropolis the only faction with two army-growth engines running simultaneously. A Rufus campaign genuinely ends fights with more units than it started, from two directions at once. (And Mag — the Arcane school-master, per the master hero table — casts every Arcane spell a level higher while locking the enemy out of the school entirely: two elite Arcane mains in one faction.)

    Avoid leaning on: Daylight's healing suite — Heavenly Blades' heal explicitly excludes undead, and the school's living-army assumptions run through several entries. Cherry-pick Radiant Armour; skip the rest.

    Hero picks¶

    • Lord Rufus (the campaign engine): Masterful Rewind Life. The double-growth loop described above; arguably the strongest campaign-economy hero in the game. (Corrected attribution — earlier drafts credited this to Mag.)
    • Mag (the school-master): +1 level to every Arcane spell, no same-school lockout, and the enemy hero cannot cast Arcane at all — the other elite Necromancer main.
    • Marl (the control general): Web specialist from battle start — pairs with the ranged skeleton core to keep melee threats permanently out of reach.
    • Laura (Masterful Despair): the stackable-DoT school's missing owner, now confirmed — her all-enemy-types version plus Sorcery start makes her the curse build's captain.
    • Shadespinner Oona (Nightshade school-master): +1 level to all Nightshade spells and the enemy can't cast the school — the debuff faction's debuff queen.
    • Bulwark: the physical-damage specialist for players who'd rather swing than cast.

    Three builds¶

    1. The Rising Tide (beginner): mass Skeleton Archers behind a Wight/Barghest screen; fight everything on the map; let Necromancy compound. The build that plays itself.

    2. The Drain Engine (mid-game): Vampire-line core + Liches, hero on Nightshade (Despair + Vulnerability). Long fights favor you twice — lifedrain sustains the front while curses grind the enemy down.

    3. Rufus's Paradox (late-game): any quality core + Lord Rufus casting Masterful Rewind Life; deliberately trade stacks knowing they return larger. The build that breaks the game's attrition math outright.

    Matchups (vs. completed chapters)¶

    Against Dungeon: the classic tempo-vs-inevitability matchup — Dungeon must win by week 4–5 (per the Dungeon chapter's own curve) because every week after belongs to the snowball. Against Temple: Fatal Decay (Nightshade, 4 mana) is Temple's scariest tool against you — it switches off both lifedrain and post-battle logic in the fights where it's cast; likewise expect Daylight cleanses to strip your curse stack. Full matchup grid: STUB.

    Common mistakes¶

    • Avoiding fights to "preserve the army" — backwards for this faction; fights are the economy.
    • Leaving Necromancy raising junk on autopilot instead of toggling Necromancer Will (see the Neutral chapter).
    • Learning Sleep or living-heal spells with no legal targets in the mirror or on your own troops.
    • Racing to T7 before the skeleton engine is running — the pyramid is built from the bottom here.

    Verdict: ★★★★☆ (4/5)¶

    The best pure campaign faction in the game — its growth loop converts time into inevitability, and Mag's Rewind Life turns that loop into a flywheel. Held from 5/5 by a slower early game than Dungeon's and a gameplan that, while dominant, is vulnerable to the small set of tools (Fatal Decay chief among them) designed specifically to switch it off.
