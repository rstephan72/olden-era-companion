    Temple — The Church of the Sun¶

    ⭐ Power: 4/5 · 🟢 Beginner Friendly: 5/5 · ⚔️ Campaign: 4/5 · 💰 Economy: 4/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 2/5 · 📈 Early 4/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 4/5

    In one sentence: The classic human faction — well-rounded troops that get extra value from buffs, a comeback-mechanic faction skill, and the game's deepest bench of Daylight spell specialists, making it the most forgiving faction in the game without being weak.

    Identity (per the official faction page): governed by the High Inquisition; humans; keywords Unit Empowerment and Strong Unit Protection. Its troops are individually unspectacular and collectively reliable — the faction's power lives in multiplying solid stats with buffs, morale, and hero support. Native terrain: creatures fight better on it (+1 Initiative; +10%/-10% damage with the Righteousness terrain subskill).

    Faction skill: Righteousness¶

    Known by all Knights and Clerics, unavailable to everyone else.

    What it does: Whenever a friendly creature stack dies or kills an enemy stack, the hero's Attack, Defense, Spellpower, and Knowledge all increase by +1 for two rounds (three at higher skill rank). Stackable.

    Why it's sneaky-strong: it pays out on both good and bad events — your kills stack it, and your losses stack it too. In a bloody even fight, a Temple hero might sit on +5 to all four stats by round three: their spells hit ~25% harder (Spellpower), their army's damage climbs (Attack), everything takes less (Defense). It's a comeback-and-snowball mechanic in one, and it rewards exactly the big-messy-battle gameplan Temple armies want anyway.

    Subskill of note: the native-terrain rank (+10% dealt / -10% taken on Temple ground) effectively makes home-turf defensive battles a 20-point damage swing before anything else happens.

    Unit roster (full stats, verified against the official wiki)¶

    Upgrade format: Base → Upgrade A / Upgrade B (choose a path per the usual dual-upgrade system).

    Tier 1: Swordsman → Guard Captain / Sun's Aegis¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd
    Swordsman 85g 12 4/4 2–3 5 4
    Guard Captain 110g 12 6/5 2–3 6 4
    Sun's Aegis 110g 15 5/8 2–3 5 5

    The fork is offense (Guard Captain: +2 Atk, +1 Init) vs. wall (Sun's Aegis: +3 HP, +4 Def, +1 Spd). Take Sun's Aegis in most campaigns — Temple's T1 job is absorbing hits while the shooters work, and 15 HP / 8 Def is outstanding tanking per gold at this tier.

    Tier 2: Crossbowman → Austringer / Marksman¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Notes
    Crossbowman 105g 10 5/3 3–4 5 Ranged
    Austringer 140g 10 5/4 3–4 5 Ranged
    Marksman 140g 10 6/3 3–4 7 Ranged, no range penalty

    Marksman is the pick and it isn't close: +2 Initiative and the removal of the distance damage falloff turns a filler shooter into a genuine core stack. No-range-penalty shooters can sit in the safest corner of the map and lose nothing.

    Tier 3: Griffin → Temple Griffin / Guardian Griffin¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd
    Griffin 255g 25 7/6 5–9 9 5
    Temple Griffin 350g 25 8/6 5–9 12 7
    Guardian Griffin 350g 25 9/10 7–7 10 6

    The faction's tempo unit. Temple Griffin at Initiative 12 acts before almost everything in the early-to-mid game — it's your opener, your shooter-hunter, and your objective-grabber. Guardian Griffin (fixed 7–7 damage, 10 Def) is the steadier attrition pick. Temple Griffin for aggression, Guardian for grind — and note the fixed damage on Guardian pairs beautifully with Masterful Blessing's max-damage philosophy without needing it.

    Tier 4: Lightweaver → Hierophant / Sun Herald¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Notes
    Lightweaver 310g 35 11/11 11–11 4 Ranged, Morale 2
    Hierophant 440g 35 13/16 11–11 6 Ranged
    Sun Herald 440g 35 16/13 11–18 5 Ranged

    The faction's backbone — and the unit that makes hero Zenith S-tier (she starts with Lightweavers in her opening army, weeks before anyone else can build them). Fixed 11–11 damage means perfectly predictable volleys; the built-in Morale 2 means bonus turns. Sun Herald (11–18, higher Attack) for damage ceilings; Hierophant (16 Def) if your Lightweavers keep getting dived.

    Tier 5: Cavalry → Noble Cavalry / Sunspear Cavalry¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd
    Cavalry 850g 85 12/17 10–14 10 7
    Noble Cavalry 1220g 85 12/17 12–16 13 7
    Sunspear Cavalry 1220g 85 14/22 12–16 10 8, Morale 2

    The hammer to the infantry's anvil. Noble Cavalry at Initiative 13 is the fastest-acting thing Temple fields until Archangels — first-strike charges that delete a shooter stack before it fires. Sunspear trades that tempo for 22 Defense and morale. Noble for alpha strikes, Sunspear for lines that hold.

    Tier 6: Inquisitor → Mother Superior / Excommunicator¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd
    Inquisitor 1000g 90 20/24 19–23 7 4
    Mother Superior 1500g 125 20/28 19–23 9 5
    Excommunicator 1500g 90 26/24 23–33 8 6

    The starkest upgrade fork in the roster: Mother Superior is a fortress (125 HP, 28 Def), Excommunicator is a blender (23–33 damage, 26 Atk). The names telegraph the play: Mother Superior anchors defensive compositions; Excommunicator ends fights.

    Tier 7: Angel → Archangel / Apotheosis¶

    Unit Cost HP Atk/Def Dmg Init Spd Notes
    Angel 2400g + 1 Gem 225 30/30 50–75 8 4 Ranged
    Archangel 4000g + 2 Gem 225 35/30 50–75 12 5 Ranged, no penalties
    Apotheosis 4000g + 2 Gem 300 30/35 50–75 10 10 Ranged

    A ranged tier 7 — one of Temple's defining luxuries. Angels deal their 50–75 without ever taking a counterattack. Archangel (Initiative 12, no range/melee penalties of any kind) is the flexible pick; Apotheosis (300 HP, Speed 10) is a flying fortress that repositions across the entire board in one move. Note the Gem cost mirrors Dungeon's dragon economy — budget Gems from mid-game.

    Abilities — the passive and Focus layer¶

    How this works (verified from first-party documentation): every unit carries up to two kinds of special abilities. Passives are always on — auras, triggers, movement types, attack-type clauses. Actives cost Focus Points, which units accumulate by attacking and being attacked; bank enough and the ability fires. This layer is where Olden Era's combat depth actually lives — two stacks with identical stat lines can play completely differently — and it's what the Focus economy tools in this book (Murmuring, Energize, Battle March, Fancy Mask, Silence) exist to accelerate or deny.

    Verified Temple abilities:

    Unit Ability Type Effect
    Griffin line Flying Passive Movement ignores obstacles and traps
    Griffin line Unlimited Retaliation Passive Never runs out of counterattacks — every melee hit against it is punished
    Marksman No Range Penalty Passive Full damage at any distance
    Archangel Sharpshooter Passive No range or melee penalties of any kind
    Angel line Resurrection Active (Focus) Revives fallen allied troops mid-battle — the ability that makes the T7 one of the best units in the game

    Focus priorities: Temple has exactly one active that matters, and it's the best one printed: bank the Angels' Focus for Resurrection. Don't leak their charges on ordinary volleys when a key stack is about to break — a timed Resurrection swings more HP than any Angel attack. The Griffins' unlimited retaliation is the passive to build around: park them where the swarm factions must engage, and every wave pays. (Per-unit ability text for the remaining lines lives on individual unit pages — tracked in the audit.)

    Recommended magic schools (cross-referenced to the magic chapters)¶

    Primary: Daylight — this is not a suggestion, it's the faction's second identity. Temple's "troops gain extra benefits from buffs" faction text is a direct pointer, and the hero roster seals it: four of the game's confirmed Masterful-Daylight/adjacent specialists are Temple Clerics — Vesper (Masterful Blessing: always max damage), Julius (Masterful Healing Water: heals + cleanses), Anastasia the Meek (Masterful Arina's Touch: +Speed), and the crossover pick Nadir (Masterful Vulnerability, a Nightshade spell — always-max-damage against a marked target). Key entries to read in the Daylight chapter: Blessing and Favorable Wind (your Marksmen/Lightweavers love them), Radiant Armour (the L4 army-wide -35% turns Temple's durable line unkillable), and Judgement for the late-game nuke Righteousness's stacking Spellpower quietly enables.

    Secondary: Arcane for Rewind Life — Temple's whole gameplan is preserving a quality army, and the resurrection spell is the strongest preservation tool in the game.

    Situational tech: Nadir makes a genuine hybrid possible — Daylight buffs on your army, Masterful Vulnerability on their key stack, then concentrated fire. It's the single strongest cross-school package any faction gets natively.

    Hero picks¶

    • Zenith (S-tier pick): starts with Lightweavers — a T4 core weeks early — plus a specialization that boosts their production and stats. The tempo advantage compounds all game.
    • Vesper / Julius / Anastasia the Meek (Clerics): the Masterful Daylight trio; pick whichever spell matches your plan (damage consistency / sustain / tempo respectively).
    • Nadir (contrarian pick): rated poorly by tier lists for anti-synergy (a Nightshade skill in the buff faction), but Masterful Vulnerability is the best single-target kill enabler in the game — as a second hero supporting a might main, he's excellent.
    • Lord Edgar: starts with Advanced Righteousness and flexible skill slots — the subclass-chaser's pick.

    Three builds¶

    1. The Sunlit Wall (beginner): Sun's Aegis + Guardian Griffins + Hierophants + Mother Superior front; Marksmen + Lightweavers behind. Cleric hero casting Radiant Armour and Healing Water. Wins by being unkillable while ranged damage accumulates. Weakness: slow to close out.

    2. Dawn Lance (tempo): Temple Griffins + Noble Cavalry + Excommunicators, hero with Haste/Blessing (or Mila-style speed support). Alpha-strikes the enemy's ranged core before it acts. Weakness: stalls against heavy walls.

    3. Chosen of the Sun (late-game): Archangels/Apotheosis + Sun Heralds + Excommunicators; hero stacking Spellpower via Righteousness into Judgement/Rewind Life. The "quality over quantity" endgame Temple's Gem economy naturally builds toward.

    Matchups (vs. the Dungeon pilot chapter)¶

    Temple's disciplined, buffed line is a solid answer to Dungeon's tempo: Marksmen out-range Onyx Dancers with no falloff, and Radiant Armour blunts the Dancer volley spam. Dungeon's counterplay is Black-Dragon-era spell immunity (your buffs don't help what they can't touch) and stance-swung burst. Full matchup grid: STUB — pending the factions category's matchup pass.

    Common mistakes¶

    • Skipping buffs "to save mana" — an unbuffed Temple army is just an average army; the faction's whole margin lives in the multipliers.
    • Taking Guard Captain over Sun's Aegis by default.
    • Forgetting Angels are ranged and marching them into melee.
    • Under-using Righteousness's Spellpower stacks — late-fight Cleric nukes hit far harder than the same spell cast on round one.

    Verdict: ★★★★☆ (4/5)¶

    The best faction to learn the game with and still a top-tier campaign pick for veterans — forgiving units, a self-correcting faction skill, and the deepest native synergy with the game's premier buff school. Docked one star only because its skill ceiling arrives early: Temple rarely loses to its own complexity, but it also has fewer broken-in-the-right-hands tricks than Dungeon or Grove.
