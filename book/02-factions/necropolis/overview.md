# Necropolis — Shadowspire's Endless Dead

> ⭐ Power: 4/5 · 🟢 Beginner Friendly: 4/5 · ⚔️ Campaign: 5/5 ·
> 💰 Economy: 4/5 · 🧠 Skill Ceiling: 3/5 ·
> 📈 Early 3/5 · Mid 4/5 · Late 5/5

**In one sentence:** The attrition faction — every battle you win
makes your army *bigger* through Necromancy, your units drain strength
from enemies as they fight, and given enough time you simply cannot be
out-grown.

**Identity (per the official faction page):** ruled by the
Necromancers' Guild of Shadowspire; undead; keywords *Strong Debuffs &
Curses* and *Improving army by raising the Dead*. Community consensus
rates it the best late-game faction and, alongside Dungeon, one of the
two easiest to win with — the snowball takes care of itself.

***

## Faction skill: Necromancy

Known by all Necropolis hero classes (Death Knights and their magic
counterparts), unavailable elsewhere.

**What it does (official mechanics, verified):** After winning a
battle, **transforms 10/15/20% (by skill rank) of the strongest enemy stack
into friendly Undead of the same tier.** This consumes Necromantic
Energy, which stores up to 1,000–1,500 (by rank) and refills each week (or when the hero has no stack
of that tier). Crucially, **Necromancy works on all creatures, not just
the Undead** — every living enemy is future army. Documented subskills
include: native-terrain bonus (+10% dealt / -10% taken on Deathland),
+4 mana per battle victory, and a granted ability that heals a friendly
Undead stack **and revives its fallen units**. The Necromancer class
additionally spawns a temporary friendly Wight stack whenever an enemy
stack dies in battle.

**The companion tool — Necromancer Will (Neutral spell, 0 mana):**
toggles raising off, banking *Necromantic Energy* instead. This is the
skill's hidden depth: raising junk-tier troops dilutes a refined army,
so experienced players toggle off against low-value enemies and bank
the energy for better conversions. See the Neutral Magic chapter's
entry — it's rated 5/5 *for this faction specifically*.

**Why it defines the faction:** every other faction pays gold to grow;
Necropolis grows by fighting. The correct Necropolis campaign is a
deliberately *high-volume* one — take every fight the map offers,
because each one is a recruitment event.

## Recommended magic schools (cross-referenced)

**Primary: Nightshade.** The faction text says "Strong Debuffs &
Curses" and the school delivers exactly that; better, undead immunity
to several Nightshade tools (Sleep can't target your own troops in
mirror situations) means you suffer the school's downsides less than
anyone. Key entries: Despair (stackable DoT — undead armies grind long
fights where it shines), Vulnerability, and **Web — specialist hero
Marl (Death Knight) applies his from the start of battle**, the
perfect screen for a skeleton-archer core.

**Primary co-pick: Arcane, for one spell — Rewind Life.** **Lord
Rufus's Masterful version revives units beyond their original
amount**, which on top of Necromancy makes Necropolis the only faction
with *two* army-growth engines running simultaneously. A Rufus
campaign genuinely ends fights with more units than it started, from
two directions at once. (And Mag — the *Arcane school-master*, per the
master hero table — casts every Arcane spell a level higher while
locking the enemy out of the school entirely: two elite Arcane mains
in one faction.)

**Avoid leaning on:** Daylight's healing suite — Heavenly Blades'
heal explicitly excludes undead, and the school's living-army
assumptions run through several entries. Cherry-pick Radiant Armour;
skip the rest.

## Hero picks

- **Lord Rufus (the campaign engine):** Masterful Rewind Life. The
  double-growth loop described above; arguably the strongest
  campaign-economy hero in the game. *(Corrected attribution — earlier
  drafts credited this to Mag.)*
- **Mag (the school-master):** +1 level to every Arcane spell, no
  same-school lockout, and the enemy hero cannot cast Arcane at all —
  the other elite Necromancer main.
- **Marl (the control general):** Web specialist from battle start —
  pairs with the ranged skeleton core to keep melee threats permanently
  out of reach.
- **Laura (Masterful Despair):** the stackable-DoT school's missing
  owner, now confirmed — her all-enemy-types version plus Sorcery
  start makes her the curse build's captain.
- **Shadespinner Oona (Nightshade school-master):** +1 level to all
  Nightshade spells and the enemy can't cast the school — the debuff
  faction's debuff queen.
- **Bulwark:** the physical-damage specialist for players who'd rather
  swing than cast.

## Three builds

**1. The Rising Tide (beginner):** mass Skeleton Archers behind a
Wight/Barghest screen; fight everything on the map; let Necromancy
compound. The build that plays itself.

**2. The Drain Engine (mid-game):** Vampire-line core + Liches, hero
on Nightshade (Despair + Vulnerability). Long fights favor you twice —
lifedrain sustains the front while curses grind the enemy down.

**3. Rufus's Paradox (late-game):** any quality core + Lord Rufus
casting Masterful Rewind Life; deliberately trade stacks knowing they
return larger. The build that breaks the game's attrition math
outright.

## Matchups (vs. completed chapters)

Against **Dungeon:** the classic tempo-vs-inevitability matchup —
Dungeon must win by week 4–5 (per the Dungeon chapter's own curve)
because every week after belongs to the snowball. Against **Temple:**
Fatal Decay (Nightshade, 4 mana) is Temple's scariest tool *against
you* — it switches off both lifedrain and post-battle logic in the
fights where it's cast; likewise expect Daylight cleanses to strip
your curse stack. Full matchup grid: STUB.

## Common mistakes
- Avoiding fights to "preserve the army" — backwards for this faction;
  fights *are* the economy.
- Leaving Necromancy raising junk on autopilot instead of toggling
  Necromancer Will (see the Neutral chapter).
- Learning Sleep or living-heal spells with no legal targets in the
  mirror or on your own troops.
- Racing to T7 before the skeleton engine is running — the pyramid is
  built from the bottom here.

## Verdict: ★★★★☆ (4/5)
The best pure campaign faction in the game — its growth loop converts
time into inevitability, and Mag's Rewind Life turns that loop into a
flywheel. Held from 5/5 by a slower early game than Dungeon's and a
gameplan that, while dominant, is vulnerable to the small set of tools
(Fatal Decay chief among them) designed specifically to switch it off.
